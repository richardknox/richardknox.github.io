Keep It Simple

Why we should be mindful of complexity in A/B test design

Note: this blog post is a work in progress. I’ve decided to publish even incomplete posts, in order to gain a bit of momentum with my writing. This post will likely change over the next few days. (First posted on 24/09/2020).

A/B testing is a powerful technique. If we have enough traffic, it allows us to generate data on how our customers will react to changes on our platform. But we can suffer from test creep in a similar way to feature creep creep in product development.

I would like to discuss some common drawbacks I see in a testing first organisation, and how to deal with them.

What is A/B testing?

In a nutshell, A/B testing is taking a group of users and splitting them into two groups. With one group (control) we leave things exactly as they were before. With the other (treatment) we make a change to the user experience.

We can observe metrics about the behaviour of these two groups, for example click through rate on a button, to see what effect the experience change has on user behaviour.

Test design

In an ideal world, one where we have unlimited users and no restrictions on what we test, designing our A/B tests would be simple.

But, most of us don’t operate in an ideal world. We have restrictions placed on us, whether it’s business restrictions, derisking release of new products or even just lack of scale.

One behaviour I’ve witnessed over and over is the tendency to overcomplicate testing by adding more and more test groups.

It is easy to sit in a meeting discussing a new feature and decide on five different variants that you want to test without considering the complexity that comes with this choice. There is the obvious complexity, around traffic share, significance calculation and so on, but there is also a hidden complexity that comes with it.

Design with testing in mind

To truly effectively test, I believe you need to design your feature with testability in mind. It’s no good getting to done status on a project, only to discover that the way things work will make it incredibly difficult to setup a meaningful test.

Testing is not the goal

Why do we run tests? To validate a hypothesis we have around our users behaviour. For example, feature X will raise conversion rate by Y%.

It is simple enough to state this, but I often see people lose sight of the fact that testing is all about validating our hypothesis.

It is well known that some of the largest, and fastest growing, companies in the world run 100s and 1,000s of experiments on their user base. Booking.com is often cited as one of these, with the site regularly running 1,000 concurrent experiments. But, this can lead to a mindset where the number of tests is a metric teams are measured on, and therefore becomes a target.

As Goodhardt’s Law tells us, this is a bad thing. When we begin to strive for a certain number of tests, then it is easy to lose sight of the why we are testing.

From personal experience, when we push to increase test frequency, tests become poorly designed or rushed out in order to hit this golden number (be it 1 or 100 tests a week).

We must be careful to not confuse correlation with causation here. Just because Twitter growth increased XXX% when they hit 50 tests a week, does not mean our business will follow suit.
